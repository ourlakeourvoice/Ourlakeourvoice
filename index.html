What Is Happening

A recorded land option covering approximately 1,186 acres near Key Ranch Road, Trinidad, Texas (Henderson County) was filed in Henderson County deed records on August 20, 2025. The purchaser is Gray Bear Peak Holdings, LLC (Delaware). The developer is Diode Ventures, a wholly-owned subsidiary of Black & Veatch (Overland Park, Kansas), a global engineering and construction firm with $4.7 billion in annual revenue.

Diode Ventures first approached West Cedar Creek Municipal Utility District (WCC MUD) on April 15, 2025 — immediately requesting the district sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement. WCC MUD refused. When Diode subsequently attempted to meet individually with board members before any public meeting, WCC MUD’s GM Kenneth Malin insisted all discussions remain in open public meetings.

WCC MUD filed a formal raw water contract application with the Tarrant Regional Water District (TRWD) on March 25, 2026, explicitly listing the proposed use type as “Data Center.” Under community pressure, the WCC MUD board passed a temporary suspension on new High-Intensity Water Customer commitments on May 21, 2026. That suspension expires April 15, 2027.

On June 9, 2026, professional survey trucks from SAM LLC were observed at the Key Ranch Road site. The project is actively advancing.

Why This Matters

Cedar Creek Lake is a finite, managed reservoir owned by TRWD and supplying 2.3 million people across 11 North Texas counties. This transaction was pursued behind NDAs and shell companies with no public meeting, no public notice, and no community input. Nobody asked you.

The Environmental Record

Cedar Creek Reservoir is not simply a water source under stress from drought. Texas Commission on Environmental Quality records confirm the reservoir and its tributaries are officially impaired water bodies under federal Clean Water Act standards — and have been for years. Adding industrial water withdrawals and discharge to this system makes state-required remediation harder, not easier.

⚠ Cedar Creek Reservoir Is Officially Impaired — TCEQ 2026

The TCEQ Commission adopted the 2026 Texas 303(d) List of Impaired Waters on April 1, 2026 — five weeks before Diode Ventures filed its engineering response seeking water from this same reservoir. Cedar Creek Reservoir (Segment 0818) is listed as impaired for pH in seven separate assessment units. Multiple tributaries feeding the reservoir are listed as impaired for bacterial contamination. The TCEQ Watershed Viewer classifies the Key Ranch Road site as located within the Cedar Creek Reservoir–Cedar Creek Watershed, Trinity River Basin.

What Category 5 Means

A Category 5 listing is the most serious classification in the Texas Integrated Report. It means the water body is officially impaired — not meeting water quality standards — and requires a Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) study to determine how much of each pollutant the water body can receive while still meeting standards. TCEQ regulations governing permits to discharge into 303(d)-listed water bodies require additional scrutiny under RG-194 procedures.

Three Documented Environmental Concerns From This Project

1. Industrial Wastewater Discharge Into the Trinity River

Diode Ventures’ own May 4, 2026 engineering questionnaire response confirms: “Wastewater from the Data Center shall be pre-treated prior to entering the existing WCC MUD sanitary sewer system. The waste will ultimately terminate at WCC MUD’s existing WWTP and then discharged into the Trinity River.” Industrial discharge from a natural gas data center into a river system already feeding an impaired reservoir has not been permitted, studied, or publicly reviewed.

2. Natural Gas Power Generation — Air Quality and Water Consumption

Diode’s engineering response confirms on-site natural gas power generation at 80–150 megawatts continuous. The Environmental and Energy Study Institute documents that natural gas data center facilities can cause significant local air quality deterioration. A University of California / VCU study found direct on-site generation at comparable facilities causes up to $99 million annually in health-related damages in nearby communities. Air quality permits, combustion emissions, and power generation cooling water requirements have not been publicly quantified or permitted for this project.

3. Adding Industrial Load to an Already Impaired Watershed

Cedar Creek Reservoir is already on TCEQ’s 303(d) list requiring remediation. Six tributary impairments are recent — four listed 2024 or newer. Any new industrial water withdrawal and discharge into this watershed affects the TMDL calculations TCEQ must complete to return these water bodies to compliance. The community — and TCEQ — deserve a formal environmental impact assessment before any industrial water contract is approved.

Cedar Creek Reservoir Has No Chapter 231 Protection — And Should

Texas Local Government Code Chapter 231 grants specific county watershed protection authority around numerous Texas lakes — including Lake Tawakoni, Lake Ray Roberts, Lake Alan Henry, Lake Somerville, Lake Granbury, and Falcon Lake. Every one received protection because the Texas Legislature passed a specific subchapter upon request.

Cedar Creek Reservoir — which supplies 2.3 million people, is on TCEQ’s 2026 impaired waters list, and is the current target of an industrial water contract application — has no equivalent Chapter 231 protection.

The legislative template already exists. Every Chapter 231 lake subchapter begins with findings that the lake is frequented for recreational purposes by residents from every part of the state and that orderly development of its watershed is of concern to the entire state. Cedar Creek Reservoir meets every one of those criteria — more so than many lakes already protected. This is a specific, precedented, achievable legislative ask for the 90th Legislature.

Federal Endangered Species Act — Texas Fawnsfoot Mussel

Federal Listing — June 4, 2024

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service listed the Texas fawnsfoot mussel as a threatened species under the Endangered Species Act on June 4, 2024 (89 Fed. Reg. 47388, Document 2024-11645). The species’ range expressly includes the Trinity River and the East Fork of the Trinity River — the same river system the Key Ranch Road site drains into per the TPWD Watershed Viewer.

Critical habitat was designated across 1,577 river miles in the Trinity, Brazos, Colorado, and Guadalupe river basins. The ESA designation bans development or projects that could harm the species and requires federal consultation.

TRWD is already bound by this listing. The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service confirmed that the Tarrant Regional Water District entered into a voluntary Candidate Conservation Agreement with Assurances (CCAA) specifically to conserve the Texas fawnsfoot in the Trinity River watershed. TRWD voluntarily committed to conservation actions for this species in the same watershed where they are now considering approving an industrial water contract and wastewater discharge agreement. That tension has not been publicly addressed.

Henderson County Has Already Acted

Henderson County Commissioners passed a formal resolution at a packed courthouse — the largest crowd Judge Wade McKinney had seen in his 28 years of service. Roughly three dozen residents signed up to speak. The resolution language was read into the court record by County Attorney Kenneth Strawn.

Resolution Key Passages

As reported by KETK News and Mabank Monitor · Primary source document being obtained from Henderson County Clerk

On Water Resources

The court formally expresses opposition to using open loop evaporative cooling systems or other high volume potable water consumption technologies in large scale, high continuous load data center facilities within water constrained regions absent demonstrated sustainable supply and mitigation safeguards.

On Community Safeguards

The court further opposes any data center development that fails to incorporate adequate safeguards to protect county resources and residents.

On Grid Reliability

Support for data centers is conditioned on ensuring grid reliability and protecting ratepayers from increased costs.

On Legislative Action

The resolution calls for a halt to new AI data centers pending impact assessments and urges state leadership to grant local governments more authority over infrastructure planning.

The Hill County Warning — And What They Did Next

Hill County commissioners passed a one-year moratorium on data center construction in May 2026. Within weeks, developer RCM Hill LLC filed a $100 million federal lawsuit arguing the moratorium was illegal under Texas law — because Texas counties cannot zone unincorporated land without state legislative authority. On June 4, 2026, Hill County unanimously rescinded the moratorium. One commissioner said: “Unfortunately, we tried.”

But Hill County did not simply surrender. They replaced the moratorium with something potentially more durable: a Major Industrial Development Review Policy and Checklist — a legally grounded framework that imposes mandatory disclosure and scrutiny on developers before construction can begin.

The Hill County Checklist — What It Requires

Water & Resource Disclosure: Developers must state exact daily and yearly water demand and provide documented proof of impact on local water tables and existing infrastructure.

Infrastructure & Traffic Impact: Full traffic volume modeling for construction and operational phases. Bridge capacity assessments for local county bridges.

Noise & Environmental Modeling: Decibel projections from continuous cooling systems. Economic assessment of infrastructure strain versus actual tax benefits to the county.

Mandatory Public Transparency: Large visible signage posted at the proposed site. Certified direct notifications to nearby residents. Required media outreach through local news channels.

According to legal reporting by K&L Gates (June 10, 2026), the checklist already worked — several developers who were, in Judge Brassell’s words, “maybe not the most honest” abandoned their plans rather than subject themselves to the disclosure requirements. The checklist is grounded in existing Texas statutes — unlike a moratorium, it does not ban development but requires full transparency and accountability before any project proceeds.

The Model for Henderson County and Tool

A Hill County-style disclosure and review framework is legally defensible in a way a moratorium is not. Henderson County’s resolution already calls for state legislative authority. Tool City Council is examining data center policy. A Texas Legislature that provides explicit statutory authority for county-level development review frameworks would give communities the tools they need before April 15, 2027.

The Brazoria County Precedent — Chapter 312 Is Henderson County’s Leverage

Discretionary Authority Henderson County Already Has

On March 10-11, 2026, Brazoria County Commissioners voted 5-0 unanimously to deny a reinvestment zone designation and tax abatement package for a proposed 620MW data center and natural gas plant by Nightpeak Energy. Commissioners cited resident concerns about noise, water consumption, and power demand.

On June 10, 2026, Brazoria County passed a formal resolution outlining legislative requests including setback requirements from residences, light and sound mitigation based on independent impact assessments, and development fees to repair public infrastructure.

Henderson County has the same Chapter 312 authority. Any request by Diode Ventures or their end-user client for a reinvestment zone designation or tax abatement in Henderson County is entirely discretionary. The county is not required to grant it. Brazoria County’s unanimous denial — and their resolution calling for state authority — is the directly applicable precedent.

What Diode Actually Does

Diode Ventures does not build data centers. They assemble the entitlements — land rights, water contracts, grid access, permits — and sell the completed entitled project to an undisclosed end user. Their parent company Black & Veatch, a $4.7 billion engineering firm, then serves as the construction contractor for the buyer.

This means the community of Cedar Creek Lake is not negotiating with a data center operator. They are negotiating with an infrastructure entitlement developer whose undisclosed client will ultimately own and operate whatever is built on 1,186 acres adjacent to Cedar Creek Reservoir.

How to File Your Own Texas PIA Request

Under the Texas Public Information Act (Texas Government Code Chapter 552), any person — resident or not — can request public records from any Texas government agency. It is free for requests under $40. The agency must respond within 10 business days. Here is how to do it.

Step 1
Identify the Agency and Contact
TRWD: records@trwd.com · trwd.justfoia.com
WCC MUD: relations@wccmud.com · (903) 432-3704
Henderson County: county clerk at courthouse
TCEQ: public.access@tceq.texas.gov
NTVGCD: district@ntvgcd.org
PUCT: records@puc.texas.gov
Always send by email AND certified mail for documentation.
Step 2
Write Your Request
Begin: "Pursuant to the Texas Public Information Act, Texas Government Code Chapter 552, I request copies of the following records..."

Be specific: cite document numbers, dates, named individuals, and entity names. Vague requests get clarification requests back. Name the documents you know exist. Ask for records in electronic format. State: "If cost exceeds $40 please notify me before proceeding."
Step 3
Send and Document
Email the request and send a certified mail copy the same day. Screenshot or print your email confirmation. Note the date — the agency has 10 business days to respond. If they request clarification, respond promptly in writing. Keep every communication. Your request and the response are both public records.
Step 4
What to Expect Back
The agency will either: produce records; request clarification (respond promptly); claim an exception and refer to the Texas Attorney General; or request a cost estimate over $40. If records are withheld, they must cite the specific statutory exception. You can challenge withheld records through the AG. If you receive documents — share them with info@ourlakeourvoice.com. Every new primary source strengthens the community record.

What We Don’t Know Yet

Rigorous advocacy requires honesty about the limits of current knowledge. These are the open questions our research has identified but not yet answered from primary sources. If you have information relevant to any of these questions, contact us at info@ourlakeourvoice.com.

Identity
Who Is the Beneficial Owner of Gray Bear Peak Holdings LLC?
Gray Bear Peak Holdings LLC (Delaware) holds the recorded option on Key Ranch Road. Delaware LLCs do not disclose beneficial owners publicly. Richard Winterman signed the agreement. Lear & Lear PLLC (Salt Lake City) served as legal counsel. Neither has been publicly connected to Diode Ventures or any known end user. Diode’s confirmed clients include Google and Meta.
End User
Who Is Diode’s Undisclosed Client for This Project?
Diode Ventures’ business model is to develop entitled projects and sell them to technology end users. Their Rocky Branch Creek project was described as sold to “a major technology company” before Google confirmed it as Project Mica — a $10 billion campus. The Key Ranch Road end user has not been publicly identified. The community has no right under current Texas law to know who will ultimately hold these water entitlements.
Legal
Do the Four TCWCID Covenants Bar Industrial Water Use?
Four recorded instruments in Henderson County deed records restrict water use on the Key Ranch Road parcels to irrigation and stock water only and bind all successors and assigns. Whether these covenants remain legally enforceable against a data center use is an open legal question. TRWD — the successor to TCWCID No. 1 — has not publicly addressed them. A Texas PIA to TRWD requesting their legal position on these instruments was filed June 9, 2026. Response due approximately June 24.
Federal
Has a Section 404 Jurisdictional Determination Been Requested?
Cedar Creek is a direct tributary to the Trinity River. Commercial development on or near jurisdictional waters may require a Clean Water Act Section 404 permit from the USACE Fort Worth District. A parallel TCEQ Section 401 water quality certification would also be required. Multiple federally listed species have documented Henderson County presence. No public disclosure of any federal permitting process has been made as of June 2026.
Regulatory
Has a Chapter 312 Tax Abatement Been Applied For?
Diode Ventures structured an $8.2 billion Chapter 100 incentive package for their Golden Plains project in Kansas City. Texas Chapter 312 reinvestment zones and tax abatements for this project, if applied for, would require Henderson County Commissioners Court approval — a discretionary vote the county can deny. Brazoria County voted 5-0 to deny a comparable abatement request in March 2026. No Chapter 312 application has been publicly disclosed for the Key Ranch Road project.
Legislative
Will the 90th Legislature Add Cedar Creek Reservoir to Chapter 231?
Texas Local Government Code Chapter 231 grants specific watershed protection authority to counties around numerous Texas lakes. Cedar Creek Reservoir — supplying 2.3 million people and officially listed on TCEQ’s 2026 impaired waters list — has no Chapter 231 protection. Every protected lake got there because someone asked the Legislature. Public comment requesting this protection was submitted to the Texas House Natural Resources Committee on June 23, 2026.
Environmental
Are Federally Listed Species Present at the Key Ranch Road Site?
The Texas fawnsfoot mussel (listed threatened June 4, 2024) has documented presence in Henderson County and Trinity River basin habitat. Multiple additional species of conservation concern are documented in Henderson County per TPWD records. A formal endangered species survey at the Key Ranch Road site — required as part of any Section 404 permit application — has not been publicly disclosed.
Water Rights
Can Water Contract Entitlements Transfer Without Community Notice?
Diode Ventures’ model is to develop entitled projects and sell them to undisclosed end users. If a water contract is approved for a “Data Center” use and the entitled project is subsequently sold, Texas law does not currently require public notice of that transfer or confirmation that the new owner will use the water for the same stated purpose. This is an identified regulatory gap with no current legislative remedy.
