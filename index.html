Critical Deadline

The West Cedar Creek MUD temporary suspension on new high-intensity water commitments expires April 15, 2027. Durable protection must be in place before that date. MUD board meets the 4th Monday of each month, 6 PM, 118 S. Southshore Dr, Tool TX.

What Is Happening

```

A recorded land option covering approximately 1,186 acres near Key Ranch Road, Trinidad, Texas (Henderson County) was filed August 20, 2025 in Henderson County deed records. The purchaser is Gray Bear Peak Holdings, LLC (Delaware). The developer is Diode Ventures, a wholly-owned subsidiary of Black & Veatch (Overland Park, Kansas), a global engineering and construction firm with $4.7 billion in annual revenue.

Diode Ventures approached West Cedar Creek Municipal Utility District (WCC MUD) seeking up to 5 million gallons per day of water from Cedar Creek Lake — water owned by Tarrant Regional Water District (TRWD), which supplies 2.3 million people across 11 North Texas counties.

WCC MUD filed a formal raw water contract application with TRWD on March 25, 2026, explicitly listing the proposed use as "Data Center." Under community pressure, the MUD board passed a temporary suspension on new high-intensity water commitments on May 21, 2026. That suspension expires April 15, 2027.

Why This Matters to You

Cedar Creek Lake is a finite, managed reservoir. It supplies drinking water to Fort Worth, Arlington, Mansfield, and dozens of other communities. The proposed data center's 30-year buildout would draw 1.825 billion gallons per year indefinitely — a permanent reallocation of a shared public resource, negotiated behind NDAs and shell companies, without public notice or vote.

The data center would be built in three stages over 12 years. At full buildout, the facility would consume 1.825 billion gallons of Cedar Creek water per year — and that demand would continue through year 30 of the contract and beyond.

The wastewater plan: pre-treated water from the data center would enter WCC MUD's sanitary sewer system and ultimately discharge into the Trinity River. What goes into the lake as a pristine reservoir would return — if at all — as treated industrial discharge downstream.

Henderson County Has Already Acted

```

Henderson County Commissioners passed a formal resolution at a packed courthouse — the largest crowd Judge Wade McKinney had seen in his 28 years of service. Roughly three dozen residents signed up to speak. The resolution language was read into the court record by County Attorney Kenneth Strawn.

Resolution Key Passages

As reported by KETK News and Mabank Monitor · Primary source document being obtained from Henderson County Clerk

On Water Resources

The court formally expresses opposition to using open loop evaporative cooling systems or other high volume potable water consumption technologies in large scale, high continuous load data center facilities within water constrained regions absent demonstrated sustainable supply and mitigation safeguards.

On Community Safeguards

The court further opposes any data center development that fails to incorporate adequate safeguards to protect county resources and residents.

On Grid Reliability

Support for data centers is conditioned on ensuring grid reliability and protecting ratepayers from increased costs.

On Legislative Action

The resolution calls for a halt to new AI data centers pending impact assessments and urges state leadership to grant local governments more authority over infrastructure planning.

The Hill County Warning

Hill County commissioners passed a one-year moratorium on data center construction in May 2026. Within weeks, developer RCM Hill LLC filed a federal lawsuit arguing the moratorium was illegal under Texas law — because Texas counties lack broad zoning authority over unincorporated land. On June 4, 2026, Hill County unanimously rescinded the moratorium under legal pressure. One commissioner said simply: "Unfortunately, we tried."

This is why Henderson County's resolution calls for state legislative action rather than a county moratorium — and why the Texas Legislature's June 23, 2026 hearing on data center water use is critically important. State authority is the only durable protection available to communities like Cedar Creek Lake.

Sources: KETK News · Mabank Monitor · CBS19 · KLTV · Full resolution text pending from Henderson County Clerk.

Recorded Water Covenants

```

Henderson County deed records contain four agreements between the Tarrant County Water Control and Improvement District No. 1 (predecessor to TRWD) and prior Key Ranch Road landowners. These covenants are listed as encumbrances in the current chain of title (Instrument No. 2025-00018559) and run with the land, binding all successors and assigns.

What the Covenants Restrict

The recorded agreements restrict water use on these parcels to irrigation and stock water only, prohibit sale of water to third parties, and bind all successors and assigns. They arose from litigation (Case No. 64-195, Third Judicial District, Henderson County) over damages caused by Cedar Creek dam construction.

The Four Recorded Instruments

Volume 588, Page 495 · Volume 588, Page 543 · Volume 597, Page 36 · Volume 1061, Pages 135 and 144 — Henderson County Deed Records

Gray Bear Peak's attorneys are aware these covenants exist — they are listed in Exhibit B of the recorded deed. The unanswered public question: Has TRWD been asked whether these covenants bar industrial water use on this land? A Texas Public Information Act request to TRWD covering this question has been filed by community members.

Community advocates note the covenants as documented encumbrances raising unresolved questions. Determinations of current legal enforceability require qualified legal counsel.

The Groundwater Gap

```

The WCC MUD suspension addresses surface water from Cedar Creek Lake. It does not address groundwater. Under Texas's "rule of capture," a landowner may pump groundwater beneath their land with limited restriction. A data center drilling its own wells on the 1,186-acre parcel would bypass the MUD, TRWD, and the recorded covenants entirely.

Henderson County falls within the Pineywoods Groundwater Conservation District (PGCD). Community members are monitoring PGCD for any well permit applications on or near the Key Ranch Road parcels. No such applications have been publicly identified as of this writing.

The Carrizo-Wilcox Aquifer — the same formation underlying this area — is the subject of active and contested groundwater permit litigation in adjacent Anderson County. The groundwater pathway remains open and unaddressed by current public regulatory action.

```

You Are Not Alone

Texas communities across the state are grappling with data center water demands. In each case, the question is the same: who decides how a finite public resource is allocated — and who was in the room when it happened?

```
Texas Precedents

Brazoria County — Commissioners Court voted 5-0 to oppose a hyperscale data center water agreement after community pressure.

San Marcos — City Council voted 5-2 to reject a data center water contract citing long-term supply concerns.

Hood County — Enacted a moratorium on large industrial water use pending regional study.

In each case, organized community voices presenting documented facts changed the outcome. The water suspension at WCC MUD was itself a response to community pressure after this transaction was discovered through public records research.
