Critical Deadline — April 15, 2027

The West Cedar Creek MUD temporary suspension on new high-intensity water commitments expires on this date. Durable protection must be in place before then. The groundwater moratorium expires six months earlier on October 1, 2026. WCC MUD board meets the 4th Monday of each month, 6 PM, 118 S. Southshore Dr, Tool TX.

What Is Happening

A recorded land option covering approximately 1,186 acres near Key Ranch Road, Trinidad, Texas (Henderson County) was filed in Henderson County deed records on August 20, 2025. The purchaser is Gray Bear Peak Holdings, LLC (Delaware). The developer is Diode Ventures, a wholly-owned subsidiary of Black & Veatch (Overland Park, Kansas), a global engineering and construction firm with $4.7 billion in annual revenue.

Diode Ventures first approached West Cedar Creek Municipal Utility District (WCC MUD) on April 15, 2025 — immediately requesting the district sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement. WCC MUD refused. When Diode subsequently attempted to meet individually with board members before any public meeting, WCC MUD’s GM Kenneth Malin insisted all discussions remain in open public meetings.

WCC MUD filed a formal raw water contract application with the Tarrant Regional Water District (TRWD) on March 25, 2026, explicitly listing the proposed use type as “Data Center.” Under community pressure, the WCC MUD board passed a temporary suspension on new High-Intensity Water Customer commitments on May 21, 2026. That suspension expires April 15, 2027.

On June 9, 2026, professional survey trucks from SAM LLC were observed at the Key Ranch Road site. The project is actively advancing.

Why This Matters

Cedar Creek Lake is a finite, managed reservoir owned by TRWD and supplying 2.3 million people across 11 North Texas counties. This transaction was pursued behind NDAs and shell companies with no public meeting, no public notice, and no community input. Nobody asked you.

Impacts That Remain Regardless of Cooling Method

No Matter the Cooling Method

Industrial Noise: Air-cooled systems are louder, not quieter — continuous low-frequency hum 24/7/365.

Permanent Light Pollution: High-intensity perimeter security lighting continuously — permanently altering dark-sky character.

ERCOT Grid Stress: 80–150 megawatts of continuous power draw increases outage probability during peak heat demand.

Power Generation Water: On-site natural gas power generation consumes additional water not yet publicly quantified.

Property Values: Real estate agents must disclose known industrial developments. “Peaceful lakeside community” becomes “lake near data center.”

Three Water Pathways

The WCC MUD temporary suspension addresses only one of three potential water pathways. The community must watch all three.

⚠ Temporarily Blocked — Expires Apr 15, 2027
Pathway 1 — Surface Water via WCC MUD
WCC MUD filed formal TRWD application March 25, 2026 for “Data Center” use. Suspension blocks this path until April 15, 2027. TRWD stands to receive ~$9.3M in buy-in premiums — a financial incentive for the lake’s owner to approve when suspension expires.
👁 Unblocked — Watch TRWD
Pathway 2 — Direct TRWD Raw Water Contract
Developer can bypass WCC MUD entirely and approach TRWD directly. No moratorium blocks this. Four recorded covenants restricting water use on this land have not been publicly addressed by TRWD.
🔒 Temporarily Blocked — Oct 1, 2026
Pathway 3 — Private Groundwater Wells
NTVGCD moratorium (May 21, 2026) and permanent court injunction (Anderson County, Oct 23, 2025) block permits over 3,000 ac-ft/year until three mandatory studies complete. Groundwater reopens six months before the MUD suspension.
The Gap That Matters

The groundwater moratorium expires October 1, 2026 — six months before the WCC MUD suspension expires April 15, 2027. That six-month window is currently unaddressed by any community protective action.

Henderson County Has Already Acted

Henderson County Commissioners passed a formal resolution at a packed courthouse — the largest crowd Judge Wade McKinney had seen in his 28 years of service. Roughly three dozen residents signed up to speak. The resolution language was read into the court record by County Attorney Kenneth Strawn.

Resolution Key Passages

As reported by KETK News and Mabank Monitor · Primary source document being obtained from Henderson County Clerk

On Water Resources

The court formally expresses opposition to using open loop evaporative cooling systems or other high volume potable water consumption technologies in large scale, high continuous load data center facilities within water constrained regions absent demonstrated sustainable supply and mitigation safeguards.

On Community Safeguards

The court further opposes any data center development that fails to incorporate adequate safeguards to protect county resources and residents.

On Grid Reliability

Support for data centers is conditioned on ensuring grid reliability and protecting ratepayers from increased costs.

On Legislative Action

The resolution calls for a halt to new AI data centers pending impact assessments and urges state leadership to grant local governments more authority over infrastructure planning.

The Hill County Warning

Hill County commissioners passed a one-year moratorium on data center construction in May 2026. Within weeks, developer RCM Hill LLC filed a federal lawsuit arguing the moratorium was illegal under Texas law. On June 4, 2026, Hill County unanimously rescinded the moratorium. One commissioner said: “Unfortunately, we tried.”

This is why Henderson County’s resolution calls for state legislative action rather than a county moratorium — and why the Texas Legislature’s June 23, 2026 hearing on data center water use is critically important.

Sources: KETK News · Mabank Monitor · CBS19 · KLTV · Full resolution text pending from Henderson County Clerk.

Recorded Water Covenants

Henderson County deed records contain four agreements between the Tarrant County Water Control and Improvement District No. 1 (predecessor to TRWD) and prior Key Ranch Road landowners. These covenants are listed as encumbrances in the current chain of title and run with the land, binding all successors and assigns.

What the Covenants Restrict

The recorded agreements restrict water use on these parcels to irrigation and stock water only, prohibit sale of water to third parties, and bind all successors and assigns. Gray Bear Peak’s attorneys are aware — they are listed in Exhibit B of the recorded deed.

The Four Recorded Instruments

Volume 588, Page 495 · Volume 588, Page 543 · Volume 597, Page 36 · Volume 1061, Pages 135 and 144 — all Henderson County Deed Records.

The Unanswered Public Question

Has TRWD been asked whether these covenants bar industrial water use on this land? A Texas Public Information Act request covering this question has been filed with TRWD. Response due approximately June 24, 2026.

Note: Community advocates note the covenants as documented encumbrances raising unresolved questions. Determinations of current legal enforceability require qualified legal counsel.
