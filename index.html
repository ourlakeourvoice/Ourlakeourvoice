Critical Deadline — April 15, 2027

The West Cedar Creek MUD temporary suspension on new high-intensity water commitments expires on this date. Durable protection must be in place before then. The groundwater moratorium expires six months earlier on October 1, 2026. WCC MUD board meets the 4th Monday of each month, 6 PM, 118 S. Southshore Dr, Tool TX.

What Is Happening

A recorded land option covering approximately 1,186 acres near Key Ranch Road, Trinidad, Texas (Henderson County) was filed in Henderson County deed records on August 20, 2025. The purchaser is Gray Bear Peak Holdings, LLC (Delaware). The developer is Diode Ventures, a wholly-owned subsidiary of Black & Veatch (Overland Park, Kansas), a global engineering and construction firm with $4.7 billion in annual revenue.

Diode Ventures first approached West Cedar Creek Municipal Utility District (WCC MUD) on April 15, 2025 — immediately requesting the district sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement. WCC MUD refused. When Diode subsequently attempted to meet individually with board members before any public meeting, WCC MUD's GM Kenneth Malin insisted all discussions remain in open public meetings.

WCC MUD filed a formal raw water contract application with the Tarrant Regional Water District (TRWD) on March 25, 2026, explicitly listing the proposed use type as "Data Center."

Under community pressure, the WCC MUD board passed a temporary suspension on new High-Intensity Water Customer commitments on May 21, 2026. That suspension expires April 15, 2027.

On June 9, 2026, professional survey trucks from SAM LLC — a national survey and mapping firm — were observed at the Key Ranch Road site. Professional survey work precedes deed transfer, financing, or permit applications. The project is actively advancing.

```
Why This Matters

Cedar Creek Lake is a finite, managed reservoir owned by TRWD and supplying 2.3 million people across 11 North Texas counties including Fort Worth and Arlington. This transaction was pursued behind NDAs and shell companies with no public meeting, no public notice, and no community input. Nobody asked you.

Impacts That Remain Regardless of Cooling Method

Diode's engineering response indicates dry-cooling technology — meaning the direct operational water draw is lower than initially stated. However, dry cooling does not eliminate the following impacts:

No Matter the Cooling Method

Industrial Noise: Air-cooled systems are louder, not quieter — continuous low-frequency hum 24/7/365, traveling farther over open water with no effective mitigation.

Permanent Light Pollution: High-intensity perimeter security lighting continuously — permanently altering the dark-sky character that defines Cedar Creek Lake communities.

ERCOT Grid Stress: 80-150 megawatts of continuous power draw increases outage probability during peak heat demand — when lake residents need air conditioning most.

Power Generation Water: Diode's own engineering response confirms on-site natural gas power generation. Water consumed cooling that generation system has not been publicly quantified.

Property Values & Narrative: Real estate agents are legally required to disclose known industrial developments. "Peaceful lakeside community" becomes "lake near data center" — regardless of physical mitigation.

Three Water Pathways

The WCC MUD temporary suspension addresses only one of three potential water pathways for this development. The community must watch all three.

```
⚠ Temporarily Blocked — Expires Apr 15, 2027
Pathway 1 — Surface Water via WCC MUD
WCC MUD filed formal TRWD application March 25, 2026 for "Data Center" use. Suspension blocks this path until April 15, 2027. TRWD stands to receive ~$9.3M in buy-in premiums — a financial incentive for the lake's owner to approve when the suspension expires.
👁 Unblocked — Watch TRWD
Pathway 2 — Direct TRWD Raw Water Contract
Developer can bypass WCC MUD entirely and approach TRWD directly for a raw water contract. The MUD suspension does not prevent this. Four recorded covenants restricting water use on this land have not been publicly addressed by TRWD. A Texas PIA request covering this question has been filed.
🔒 Temporarily Blocked — Oct 1, 2026
Pathway 3 — Private Groundwater Wells
NTVGCD Resolution 2026-001 (May 21, 2026) halts new drilling permits through at least October 1, 2026. A permanent court injunction (Anderson County, Oct 23, 2025) blocks permits over 3,000 acre-feet/year until three mandatory studies complete. Groundwater pathway reopens six months before the MUD suspension.
The Gap That Matters

The groundwater moratorium expires October 1, 2026 — six months before the WCC MUD suspension expires April 15, 2027. That six-month window is currently unaddressed by any community protective action.

Henderson County Has Already Acted

```
✅ Henderson County Commissioners Court Resolution — May 2026

Henderson County Commissioners passed a formal resolution at a packed meeting — the largest crowd Judge Wade McKinney had seen in 28 years. Roughly three dozen residents signed up to speak. The resolution language, read into the court record by County Attorney Kenneth Strawn, states:

Resolution Key Passages — As Reported by KETK News and Mabank Monitor

On Water: The court "formally expresses opposition to using open loop evaporative cooling systems or other high volume potable water consumption technologies in large scale, high continuous load data center facilities within water constrained regions absent demonstrated sustainable supply and mitigation safeguards."

On Safeguards: The court "further opposes any data center development that fails to incorporate adequate safeguards to protect county resources and residents."

On Grid Reliability: Support for data centers is conditioned on "ensuring grid reliability and protecting ratepayers from increased costs."

On Legislative Action: The resolution calls for a halt to new AI data centers pending impact assessments and urges state leadership to grant local governments more authority over infrastructure planning.

⚠️
The Hill County Warning — What Happens When Counties Go Further

Hill County commissioners passed a one-year moratorium on data center construction in May 2026. Within weeks, developer RCM Hill LLC filed a federal lawsuit arguing the moratorium was illegal under Texas law — because Texas counties lack broad zoning authority over unincorporated land. On June 4, 2026, Hill County unanimously rescinded the moratorium under legal pressure. One commissioner said simply: "Unfortunately, we tried." This is why Henderson County's resolution calls for state legislative action rather than a county moratorium — and why the Texas Legislature's June 23 hearing on data center water use matters so much.

Note: Full resolution text is pending formal online upload by Henderson County. Key passages above are sourced to KETK News and Mabank Monitor reporting on the court meeting. Primary source document being obtained from Henderson County Clerk.

Recorded Water Covenants

Henderson County deed records contain four agreements between the Tarrant County Water Control and Improvement District No. 1 (predecessor to TRWD) and prior Key Ranch Road landowners. These covenants are listed as encumbrances in the current chain of title (HC Instrument No. 2025-00018559) and run with the land, binding all successors and assigns — including Gray Bear Peak Holdings LLC.

```
What the Covenants Restrict

The recorded agreements restrict water use on these parcels to irrigation and stock water only, prohibit sale of water to third parties, and bind all successors and assigns. They arose from litigation (Case No. 64-195, Third Judicial District, Henderson County) over damages caused by Cedar Creek dam construction. Gray Bear Peak's attorneys are aware they exist — they are listed in Exhibit B of the recorded deed.

The Four Recorded Instruments

All four instruments are recorded in Henderson County Deed Records:

Volume 588, Page 495 · Volume 588, Page 543 · Volume 597, Page 36 · Volume 1061, Pages 135 and 144

The Unanswered Public Question

Has TRWD been asked whether these covenants bar industrial water use on this land? A Texas Public Information Act request covering this question has been filed with TRWD. TRWD has 10 business days to respond. Any response — including a request for covenant release or modification — becomes part of the public record already requested.

Note: Community advocates note the covenants as documented encumbrances raising unresolved questions. Determinations of current legal enforceability require qualified legal counsel.
